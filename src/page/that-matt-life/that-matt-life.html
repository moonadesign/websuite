Fidelity of desire

I want to take a moment to express what makes this project unique.

This is redundant with an entry in the glossary that I shipped with what I refer to as fidelity of desire, which is inspired by a Dustin Curtis post where he hypothesized that the best way to predict the future is to think about desire.

It was called I want, and he basically did 20 phrases all starting with I want, and it was optimizations to his current life.

For example, he wanted gigabit internet anywhere in the world for less than $100 a month.

Then he said for anything that he might need, without spending much time, the best balance of price and function and aesthetic, for a toaster or socks or deodorant or whatever it is, would be available for him to order at one click without having to spend a bunch of time researching and cost comparing and all of that.

So those are a couple examples, but the idea is he's saying I want, and putting that into words.

Where he did 20 examples that were kind of abstract and could involve any number of technologies, this kind of takes that same concept and it increases fidelity to say, if I have an AI doing work on my behalf, A, let me define the work worth doing, but B, let me do it again for every minute of every day and to do that forever, potentially, right?

And if it's worth doing, how would you know? All this got to be tested and iterated, of course, but this is the idea that's increasing the fidelity of desire, and I think what makes this project unique and special is, I tend to have a rare perspective on things because of my background in computer science and my degree in art and just my sheer uncommon gumption and will to exert this level of clarity and awareness and control over answering all these questions, getting them answered and doing them at a depth that feels worthwhile.

My career coach, Kuda, likes to say, how you do anything is how you do everything.

I feel like this is the next manifestation of creating an artifact that represents all the things I believe are worth caring about and being thoughtful about and being creative with and executing on to bring about a greater quality of life, which is dependent on access to resources, deep, warm, rich relationships with people, and a sense of accomplishment and time well spent every single day.

I would love for this to help offset some of that and get me further ahead when I'm getting started every single morning, and if I can do those things, it's the gains will not only be outsized, it'll be, it'll change my life.

That's the belief. How can I change someone else's life if I can't change my own? And how can I change a day unless I can change an hour? And how can I change an hour unless I can change a minute? Every minute matters. Let's make this thing.

Minimum viable minute loop

  1. Trigger: the minute is now (this minute, not the other 1,439).
  2. Check planning status for the active bucket; create plan if missing (can defer execution up to 20 minutes).
  3. Load required data for this minute's target.
  4. Run Matt-grade reasoning subflow: review content, generate options, make decision, proceed.
  5. Execute the selected action for now (private, collaborative, or public), or explicitly defer.
  6. Write results and queue any optional follow-on tasks only if selected by the minute's plan.

Minimum viable day loop (1,440 triggers)

  • 250 minutes: Rotango people auto dials, pings, birthdays (mixes with 364).
  • 364 minutes: Bigfoot planning, MB-influenced, one LLM-decided research thread per day.
  • 100 minutes: Topical expertise across theology, nutrition, fitness, sports, technology, and business.
  • 480 minutes: Standard work day minutes.

Minute check: 250 + 364 + 100 + 480 = 1,194, leaving a 246-minute buffer in a 1,440-minute day.

Rotango example (two zoom levels)

Zoom 1: 250-minute Rotango slot

Rotango is one person per minute for up to 250 minutes. Each minute decides the next best action for that person, not a generic batch split.

  • Start with contact inventory questions: do we have all contacts and are there new contacts.
  • Run source checks for new contacts (notes, email, Slack, and other data sources).
  • Treat each source-check as a minute-level operation in the same daily sequence.
  • Keep selection explicit: who belongs in today's 250-minute pass.
  • Do not separate enrichment into a pre-pass; enrichment is part of the pass.
  • Next-best action can vary by person/minute: research, enrichment, invitation, encouragement, voice session, gift idea capture, or social behavior analysis.
  • Scheduling logic is primarily frequency-based cadence (when), with contact type (personal/professional) as a secondary constraint.
  • Planning phases: up to 20 minutes per major bucket, plus optional up to 20-minute meta planning from the daily buffer.
  • Use steady-drip execution: do some work daily, then iterate and re-run.

Zoom 2: Single-contact loop (Aaron)

Example reasoning chain for one person: last conversation recency, current life/work context, support needs, meeting invitations, schedule availability, best send mechanism, contact preference/defaults, and missing data resolution.

  1. Load known data from existing sources (or mark unknown).
  2. Ask tight Q/A loops for missing intelligence (email, phone, work/home context, timing).
  3. Decide next-best action with Matt-grade reasoning.
  4. Choose execute-now vs queue-later based on time and schedule constraints.
  5. Track outcomes for both person-level and batch-level optimization.
