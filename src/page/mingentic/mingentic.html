Why associate tasks with minutes?

  • Budget. Every dollar gets a job. That’s the YNAB (You Need A Budget) philosophy. It’s basically like a digital envelope system. Every dollar coming in, you know exactly where it’s going. It’s making sure every dollar coming in, every time you have money going in, every time you have money going out. It’s still very, very tedious, which is why it’s always failed for me and my wife, but that’s the first sort of inspired reason. Instead of budgeting dollars, we are budgeting time. Every minute gets a job.
  • Predictability. LLMs and agentics can behave in a way that’s not predictable. If you have a discrete sequence of “it’s gonna do these things in this order over this time span,” it just makes it more predictable. You know what’s happening and when. It serves as a natural slotting mechanism and sequencing mechanism because it’s just time. It’s just things playing out over time.
  • Simplification. Zapier, N8N, and IFTT (If This Then That) are all trigger dependent. You have a trigger, then an action. And defining a trigger can be complex and technical (listening to data events via APIs, integrations, polling, webhooks, etc.). This is a simplification mechanism. The trigger is the minute it happens. During this minute, execute this task. I would much rather know at this part of my day it’s gonna check my email, and then at this other part of my day it’s gonna check it again and see if there’s anything that needs to draft, and then it’s gonna message me 20 minutes later. That’s more understandable, predictable, and manageable than traditional automation or webhooks. It’s basically like everything’s just on a cron job instead of being enslaved to the whims of data flows. There are gonna be use cases where you need more reactive automation. This isn’t for that. This is for stabilizing one’s workday and solving for things that have happened before. It’s minimalist. Not just minutes, also minimalist.
  • Parsimony. LLMs get model choice, costs, and budget wrong when everything fires at once in parallel. If you fire everything at once in parallel, you lose the ability to balance and adjust those decisions because there’s no foresight. You don’t know when things are gonna finish. You don’t know how many tokens are gonna be used. If you’ve got 200 minutes of a sequence that’s basically doing the same thing, you start to figure out for each one what the average time and token cost was. Let’s make this optimization for the next 195. You can’t do that if everything’s happening in parallel. There’s a benefit to arbitrary slow playing that will save money.
  • Compliance. When you produce audit trails, the approachability and understandability can influence judgments and outcomes. How organized were you? How diligent were you at keeping track of what happened and when? Do you have a timestamp for each? This was mostly arbitrary, but it’s a way to make the data more steady instead of volatile. If you look at any given day, you can easily see here’s what the robots did, here’s what the humans did. At this minute, this happened. At this minute, this happened. When anybody’s doing forensics, you want the timeline. If you’re firing off dozens or hundreds of tasks in the same minute, it represents noise. The steadiness, the smoothness, the slow playing of it makes for a nicer audit trail for anything regulatory or compliance related. During this hour block, these 60 tasks. It becomes easier to talk about at scale as businesses increase their volume of agentic tasks. Chapter 14 of the AI Act in Europe goes into effect in August 2026. Compliance auditing is a critical piece of any agentic system.
  • Playback. If I need to review in an approval cycle, I’m stepping through things I can see not necessarily when they finished, but when they were triggered. I know exactly when operations started. I can review that in order at any part of any day. I know how far behind I am on review cycles based on how many minutes have occurred. I get a rough idea of success states versus automatically completed states versus error or in limbo or retry needed or approval needed. Having a more linear sequence makes for easier review cycles. It humanizes the relationship between the helper and the helped.
  • Optimization. At any moment, you can reflect on how things went in any given range and figure out how to make it better. It’s easier when there’s a discrete list of n tasks over n minutes than firing a bunch of stuff off and it all takes a different amount of time and you review at some point. That’s more nebulous and less concrete, which means you’re gonna get less scientific optimization loops. It’s much easier to measure in minutes.
  • Rate limits. When you’re doing automation, take email for example, it’s generally considered bad practice to be pounding an email server and sending dozens or hundreds in a short amount of time. The recommended best practice is 30 to 90 seconds between each auto send. I’m sure that’s true for many platforms including social media. Some platforms might be more strict. At least for us, and at least for email, we’re netting out at that optimal strike zone for not overloading APIs as we automate.
  • Every minute matters. It’s fundamental to the thing computers can do without fail, predictably every single day that a human can’t do. I’m not gonna stay up 24 hours a day forever doing all this stuff. I can fill a calendar with 1,440 tasks that run every single day. It becomes easy to reason about. If I need to swap out a few hundred of those and upgrade them or displace them with a new type of thing, I can. It starts with your first agentic minute every day and then you scale until you get a full day of agentic tasks. That goes beyond automation. It goes into its own reasoning.
